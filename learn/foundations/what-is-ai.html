What is Augmented Intelligence?

Augmented intelligence pairs your judgment with the machine's speed — and keeps you in charge. The point isn't the AI. It's what you do with the time it gives back.

Beginner · 6 min read

The one idea to keep: AI is a tool you direct. Hand it the volume; keep the judgment. Stay in charge of every decision that matters.

Start with one real afternoon

Maria runs a busy community clinic. It's Wednesday, and by Friday she owes the board a report summarising three months of patient feedback — about 400 comments. Reading and hand-tallying all 400 would eat a full day she doesn't have. Here's how she does it in an hour with augmented intelligence — and why she, not the AI, is still the author.

The task: 400 comments → a board report by Friday

What the machine does — volume

Groups the 400 comments into themes, drafts a one-line summary of each, and counts them:

Wait times — 118 comments "Patients are frustrated by long waits, especially mornings." Staff friendliness — 96 (positive) Parking — 40 Booking system — 31 …

What Maria does — judgment

Reads the themes, not 400 comments, then adds what the data can't:

Wait times — 118 comments "Patients are frustrated by long waits, especially mornings. ↳ Maria: lead with this — the board cares most. Recommend a morning triage-nurse pilot. ↳ Maria: merge "Parking" into "Access" — the model split one complaint into two.

The machine handled the 400; Maria handled the judgment — the priority call, the miscategorisation only a human with context would catch, and the recommendation the data could never make. That division of labour is augmented intelligence.

Maria's afternoon is the whole pattern in miniature: the machine compresses slow work, automates the repetitive, and clears the noise — so you can spend the reclaimed time on the decisions only you can make.

When you hand work to AI, what do you keep and what do you give away?

Hand over the volume — the repetitive, mechanical work. Keep the judgment — context, priorities, ethics, and the final call.

Augmented vs. artificial intelligence

The terms get conflated, but they're different philosophies about the machine's role. The difference is one thing: is the human still in the loop? It opens on augment — press Replace and watch the You node disappear from the chain.

Interactive explanations like this follow Bret Victor's principle that you understand what you can see and change.

Artificial Intelligence

Aims to replace human decision-making with autonomous systems — the goal is a machine that runs with no human in the loop.

Self-driving cars, automated trading, fully autonomous support bots.

Augmented Intelligence

Aims to enhance human decision-making — better tools, faster retrieval, more analytical reach. The human stays in the loop.

Maria's report. A doctor reading AI-flagged scans. A writer refining an AI draft.

The distinction changes what you optimise for: AI optimises for removing the human; augmented intelligence optimises for making the human more capable. And it matters because today's AI has real limits — it hallucinates, lacks judgment, can't explain itself, and is costly to run. Augmented intelligence designs around those limits instead of pretending they're gone.

The trap: "AI will just do it for me"

The tempting model is: hand the whole task over and collect the answer. It fails for a precise reason — the model has no judgment and can't tell you when it's wrong. In Maria's report the AI confidently mislabelled comments; only a human with context caught it. Outsource the volume, never the judgment.

The key word is assistance: you direct, you decide, you verify. The machine takes research, first drafts, data processing, and pattern-matching; you keep strategy, ethics, context, and relationships.

Augment vs. replace — what's the one thing that separates them?

Whether the human stays in the loop. Replace removes you; augment keeps you deciding and verifying.
What's the trap to avoid when handing work to AI?

Outsourcing the judgment, not just the volume — the model can't tell you when it's wrong, so you must stay in the loop to catch it.

Try it on your own work

This only clicks when you do it on something real. Take one task from this week:

  1. Write the task in one line.
  2. Split it into volume (repetitive, mechanical — could be drafted, sorted, or searched) and judgment (needs your context, ethics, taste, or final call).
  3. Decide which volume parts you'd hand to AI — and the exact point where you'd stay in the loop to verify, the way Maria did.

That split is the skill. Do it five times this week and it stops being an exercise and becomes a habit.
